"Why Are You Hiding This From Canadians?": CBC Marketplace Confronts CIBC's CEO — Then the Camera Kept Rolling

The camera was still rolling when Victor Dodig walked out. CBC Marketplace did not plan this moment. They planned to ask six questions and give CIBC the right of reply — standard procedure for investigative journalism. Instead, they captured the most-watched moment in the program's fifty-year history. 3.8 million views in five hours. The clip was removed from the official CBC YouTube within 38 minutes. By then, it had been downloaded 14,000 times.

What Dodig refused to answer on camera — 840,000 Canadians are using right now.

Eight Months. One Investigation. One Question the Banks Couldn't Answer.

Eight months ago, a CBC Marketplace producer received a package from Robert Herjavec. Not an interview request. Documents.

Herjavec did not send them as a Dragon's Den investor or a Shark Tank personality. He sent them as the founder of The Herjavec Group — Canada's largest cybersecurity firm, with over $200 million in annual revenue. A company whose clients include some of Canada's largest financial institutions. A company whose engineers work inside the systems that Canada's banks don't discuss publicly.

"I know what's on their servers," he wrote. "Because my people guard it."

The Man Who Arrived With $20

To understand why Herjavec brought those documents to Marketplace — and not somewhere else — you need to understand where he came from.

His father was imprisoned in Yugoslavia for speaking against Communism. Twenty-two times. When Robert was eight years old, the family fled. They arrived in Halifax, Nova Scotia with $20 between them. They moved to Toronto and lived in the basement of a family friend's home for eighteen months. Robert spoke no English. He worked as a waiter, a newspaper delivery boy, a debt collector.

In 1990, after being fired from a technology sales job, he started a company from his own basement. He sold it for $30 million. Then he started another. The Herjavec Group today employs 800 people globally and generates over $200 million a year.

When Robert Herjavec talks about Canadians being locked out of financial tools — he is not making an ideological argument. He is describing the country that told his family there was no room for them, and then watching his family build something that country needed.

"I didn't come to Marketplace to promote anything," he told David Common before the cameras rolled. "I came because I have documentation of something that should be publicly known. And I've been watching the banks quietly try to shut it down."

What Eight Months of Investigation Found

Marketplace's investigation began with a simple question: why do Canada's major banks offer AI-powered investment tools to clients with $500,000 or more — and nothing equivalent to anyone below that threshold?

The answer, eight months of reporting revealed, is not regulatory. It is commercial.

Canada's Big Five banks have operated algorithmic trading tools for institutional clients since 2009. These systems analyze global markets continuously, execute thousands of micro-transactions per day, and generate returns that significantly outperform traditional savings products. Access through a bank's wealth management arm requires a minimum of $500,000.

Meanwhile, according to Statistics Canada data published April 2026:

  • The top 20% of Canadians own 65.7% of the country's total wealth
  • The bottom 40% own 3%
  • Canadian household debt reached $1.77 for every dollar of disposable income — a historic high
  • 60% of all outstanding Canadian mortgages are renewing in 2025 or 2026, with fixed-rate holders facing payment increases of 15 to 20%
  • The savings rate for the bottom 20% of Canadians: negative 6.7% — they are going backwards every month

In the same period, CIBC posted record profits of $7.5 billion. The average Canadian paid 22% interest on credit card balances and received 0.5% on savings accounts.

Two years ago, independent developers built accessible versions of the same AI investment technology the banks use internally. The minimum deposit: $250. Profit Rings -registered. Fully compliant with Canadian financial regulations. Already used by 840,000 Canadians.

When Marketplace began asking questions about this technology, three major Canadian banks quietly updated their terms of service — adding language restricting transfers to "non-registered AI trading platforms."

"When someone changes the rules at the exact moment you start asking questions about them," Common says in his narration, "that is itself an answer."

The Room They Weren't Expecting

Marketplace requested an interview with CIBC six weeks before filming. The response from the communications team was cooperative: "CEO Victor Dodig is available to discuss banking innovation and the accessibility of financial services for Canadians."

They did not know Common would have eight months of documents.

They did not know Herjavec would be sitting beside him.

They did not know Herjavec had brought three printed pages — verified withdrawal records from real Canadians who were already using the platform. Names, dates, amounts. Bank account destinations.

Victor Dodig, CEO of CIBC, entered the conference room at CIBC Square in Toronto at 2:15 p.m. He left at 2:43.

The Conversation CIBC Didn't Finish

David Common: "Victor, Statistics Canada published data in April showing the bottom 40% of Canadians own 3% of the country's wealth. The top 20% own 65.7%. That gap is the widest on record. CIBC posted $7.5 billion in profit last year. What is your bank actively doing to change that statistic?"

Victor Dodig: Standard response — financial literacy programs, small business lending, accessible banking products.

Common: "Your bank charges 22% on credit card balances and pays 0.5% on savings. The spread between those two numbers represents billions of dollars extracted from the Canadians you just described as your priority. Who benefits from that spread?"

Dodig began a response about risk pricing and regulatory requirements.

Common: "Robert."

Herjavec placed three pages on the table. Face up.

Robert Herjavec: "Victor. My firm provides cybersecurity for several major Canadian financial institutions. I have worked inside your systems. I know that your bank has operated AI-powered algorithmic investment tools for institutional clients since 2009. I know the minimum entry point through your wealth management arm is $500,000. And I want to ask you directly — why can a Canadian with half a million dollars access that technology, and a Canadian with $250 cannot?"

Dodig: "That's an entirely different category of product — regulatory framework, investor sophistication requirements—"

Herjavec: "It is the same algorithm. Different price tag. And I have here" — he pointed to the first page — "verified records of 47 Canadians using an equivalent Profit Rings -registered platform at $250. A truck driver from Mississauga. A nurse from Edmonton. A retired teacher from Moncton. I personally verified these transactions. They withdrew money to RBC, TD, BMO, and CIBC accounts. Including yours."

Dodig: "I'm not going to validate unverified claims about third-party financial products—"

Common: "Victor — the platform is Profit Rings -registered. That is a verifiable fact. 840,000 Canadians are using it. That is a verifiable number. Your bank updated its terms of service to restrict transfers to AI trading platforms within days of us beginning this investigation. That is a documented fact. Is there a specific inaccuracy you'd like to address?"

Dodig looked toward the communications director seated against the wall.

The communications director did not move.

Five Seconds

For five seconds, the CEO of Canada's fifth-largest bank — a man who received $12.4 million in total compensation in 2024, while the average Canadian's mortgage payment rose 18% — said nothing.

Then he stood up. Slowly and deliberately. The way someone stands when they have decided to leave and are using the controlled movement to manage what is happening in the room.

Dodig: "I'm not going to participate in content designed to market unregulated financial products to vulnerable Canadians."

Common (quietly, without raising his voice): "The product is Profit Rings -registered, Victor. That's regulated. We've confirmed it independently."

Dodig unclipped his microphone. Placed it on the table with the specific precision of a man performing composure. Walked to the door. Did not look back.

The communications director gathered his things and followed.

The camera kept rolling. The empty chair. The three pages still on the table. The microphone.

Common, to camera, after four seconds of silence:

"We gave CIBC the right of reply. They chose not to use it."

What Herjavec Said When the Room Was Empty

After Dodig left, Herjavec looked at Common. Then at camera.

"My father was in prison twenty-two times for saying things the system didn't want said. We came to Canada with $20. We lived in a basement. I built everything without their help. And I have spent thirty years watching a system that extracts from the people who have the least and protects the people who have the most."

"This technology is not a secret. It just wasn't accessible. Now it is. You just watched what the people who profit from your not knowing it — do when someone says it out loud."

The Response That Said Everything

Within forty minutes of filming ending, CIBC's communications office sent a written statement. It described Marketplace's investigation as "irresponsible financial content" that "puts vulnerable Canadians at risk." It did not identify a single factual inaccuracy. It did not cite a specific regulatory violation. It asked Canadians to "trust established financial institutions."

That statement has been shared over 900,000 times online. The most common response: they said irresponsible. They didn't say false.

In the seventy-two hours following the broadcast:

  • Three major Canadian banks updated terms of service to restrict AI platform transfers
  • The platform's registration queue reached 40,000 in twelve hours
  • CBC received more viewer correspondence than any episode since the 2021 election night

One of the 840,000

The Marketplace team reached out to verified platform users after filming. Michael Chen, 54, truck driver from Mississauga — the man whose records Herjavec had placed on the table — agreed to speak on camera.

For four years, Michael carried $43,000 in debt across two credit cards, both at 19.99% interest. He had paid over $18,000 in interest. The balances had barely moved.

Michael: "I watched the Herjavec clip on my phone between runs. When he put those papers on the table and said my name — he didn't say my full name, but I knew it was me — I had to pull over. I couldn't drive."

Marketplace: "And now, four months later?"

Michael: "I withdrew $3,100 last month to my TD account. And $2,800 the month before. I've paid off one card completely. I'm looking at the second one differently now. For the first time in four years, I'm looking at it like something I can actually finish."
