The math in the parking lot
$138 at No Frills, no coffee cream, and somehow I felt like I was losing at life.

I sat in the lot in Oshawa for a while before I started the truck, just looking at the receipt. A hundred and thirty-eight dollars, and I'd still forgotten the coffee cream — which is a stupid thing to get worked up over, and I knew it was stupid even while I was getting worked up over it. But something has been creeping up on a lot of us around here, and it landed on me right there in the front seat: when did normal life in Ontario start feeling this heavy?

I'm 47. I've worked around GM most of my adult life, the way my dad did before me. Growing up here, the auto plants felt permanent — Tim Hortons drive-thrus packed at 5:30 in the morning, Silverados warming up in the driveways, guys heading to shift work half asleep. Nobody expected to get rich off it. But you could buy a house, raise your kids, pay your bills, and feel like tomorrow was going to look more or less like today. That last part is the part that's gone quiet.

Now it's groceries, insurance, the mortgage rate, the truck repairs, the property taxes — and then every morning another headline stacked on top of it. Tariffs, trade wars, jobs moving south, shift cuts. So when GM said Oshawa was going from three shifts to two, nobody I know was actually surprised. You could feel it coming. Less joking around at work, more talk about the overtime drying up, more people getting quiet about how much they owe. One guy sold his truck the week of the announcement — and around here, selling your truck means things are worse than anyone's saying out loud.

My wife keeps telling me we're not broke. And she's right, we're not. But I'm tired of working full-time and still feeling cornered by it.

Somewhere in there a guy from work mentioned Questrade. Said a couple of people were quietly putting a bit of money in on the side instead of just letting it sit. I barely listened — sounded like one more internet thing that works great for somebody else and somehow never for you. I didn't sign up. I left the tab open on my phone for about a week, and then another bill came in and I looked again. Before I put in a single dollar I asked to talk to an actual person, and someone called me back and explained it plain. No finance-influencer talk, no pushing. Just normal stuff I could follow.

So I started small, mostly to test it. The first thing I did was pull some of it back out into my TD account, because I still didn't trust it — and it showed up the same afternoon. That's the part that got me. Not some number going up. Just the fact that I could move my own money and it actually moved.

Groceries are still ridiculous. Ontario still feels expensive, and the news isn't getting any kinder. None of that got fixed — I won't insult you by pretending it did. But here's what changed: I stopped being the guy alone in the parking lot, doing the same math over and over and feeling like the answer was always no.

What I needed wasn't a windfall. It was one thing that was actually in my own hands.

So that's what I'd tell you — don't overthink it. Ask the questions first. Start with an amount that doesn't put you under pressure. Prove to yourself you can pull it back out, same as I did.

The receipt in the parking lot won't change. But the guy holding it can.
